### Q1. Combination of a group of species at a common place is called _____.
**Options:** a. Population | b. Community | c. Ecosystem | d. None of these
**✅ Answer: (b) Community**
> Slides: The ecological hierarchy is **Individual → Population → Community → Ecosystem → Landscape → Biosphere**. A community = multiple different species living and interacting in the same area.

**Spin-offs:**
- **(a) Population → Q:** What is the difference between a population and a community?
  **A:** **Population** = group of individuals of the **same species** in an area; **Community** = group of **different species** in the same area.
- **(c) Ecosystem → Q:** What does an ecosystem include that a community does not?
  **A:** An ecosystem includes both the **biotic community** (living) AND the **abiotic (non-living) environment** (light, oxygen, water, soil, etc.).
- **(d) None → Q:** What is Landscape Ecology?
  **A:** **Landscape ecology** = "the study of interactions between the temporal and spatial aspects of a landscape and the organisms within it"; a **landscape encompasses a larger area and several ecosystems**. Above that is the **biosphere = the whole Earth**.

### Q14. In a first-order reaction, find the relation between t₉₉% and t₉₀%.
**Options:** a. 1.5× | b. 1× | c. 3× | d. 2×
**✅ Answer: (d) t₉₉% = 2 × t₉₀%**
> Slides integrated law: \(\ln([C_0]/[C]) = kt\)
> - 90%: \([C] = 0.1[C_0]\) → \(t_{90\%} = \ln(10)/k = 2.303/k\)
> - 99%: \([C] = 0.01[C_0]\) → \(t_{99\%} = \ln(100)/k = 4.606/k = 2 \times (2.303/k)\)
> - ∴ **t₉₉% = 2 × t₉₀%**

**Spin-offs:**
- **(a) 1.5× → Q:** How long to remove 99% of CO from a room if k = 1.2/h?
  **A:** Slides: \(t = \ln(100)/1.2 = 4.605/1.2 = \mathbf{3.8 \text{ h}}\)
- **(c) 3× → Q:** What is t₉₀% in terms of t₁/₂?
  **A:** \(t_{90\%} = 2.303/k\) and \(t_{1/2} = 0.693/k\), so \(t_{90\%} \approx \mathbf{3.32 \times t_{1/2}}\).
- **(b) 1× → Q:** What makes first-order half-life unique vs. zero-order?
  **A:** Slides: For first-order, \(t_{1/2} = 0.693/k\) — **constant, independent of initial concentration**. For zero-order, \(t_{1/2} = [C_0]/2k\) — depends on initial concentration.

### S1. What are the forms and units of energy?
> Slides (Lecture 11):
> - Energy = **"the capacity or ability to do work"**
> - Forms: **chemical, radiant (light), thermal (heat), mechanical, nuclear, electrical**
> - Units: **Kilojoules (kJ)** and **Kilocalories (kcal)**; **1 kcal = 4.184 kJ**

**Spin-offs:**
- **Q:** State the 1st and 2nd Laws of Thermodynamics as given in the slides.
  **A:** **1st Law:** "energy can change forms but is not created or destroyed" — deals with **quantity**. **2nd Law:** "amount of usable energy decreases as energy changes forms / **Entropy Rules!**" — deals with **quality**.
- **Q:** What is the formal definition of Gibbs free energy from the slides?
  **A:** \(G = \sum_i \mu_i N_i = H - TS\), where H = Enthalpy, S = Entropy, T = Temperature. A process proceeds spontaneously only if **ΔG < 0**.
- **Q:** What does enthalpy represent, and when is a reaction exothermic vs. endothermic?
  **A:** Slides: **Enthalpy = energy of inter/intra-molecular bonds**. When ΔH is **negative** → **exothermic** (heat released); when ΔH is **positive** → **endothermic** (heat absorbed). ΔH is also called the **heat of reaction**.

### S5. What is Activity and Concentration in Environmental Chemistry?
> Slides (Lecture 15):
> - **Activity** = "effective concentration"; designated by **{ }** brackets
> - **Concentration** designated by **[ ]** brackets
> - Related by: **{a} = γ·[c]**, where γ = activity coefficient
> - **Ionic strength** \(\mu = \frac{1}{2}\sum_i C_i Z_i^2\) (units: mol/L)
> - Fresh water: ionic strength ≈ **0.001–0.01 M**; Ocean: ≈ **0.7 M**
> - For electrolytes at moderate ionic strengths → use **Güntelberg approximation**
> - For higher ionic strengths → use **Davies approximation**
> - **For most dilute environmental systems, activity coefficients are assumed = 1**

**Spin-offs:**
- **Q:** What are the two approaches used to evaluate a chemical's fate in Environmental Chemistry?
  **A:** Slides: **(1) Kinetic** — "deals with rate of reactions"; **(2) Equilibrium** — "deals with the final results."
- **Q:** What is chemical equilibrium?
  **A:** Slides: **"A state in which the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the backward reaction; no net change in concentrations of reactants and products."** Expressed as: \(K = [C]^c[D]^d/[A]^a[B]^b\) — K is the **equilibrium constant**.
- **Q:** For the benzene example: activity coefficient in seawater vs. freshwater?
  **A:** Slides (Example 3.2): Using the salting-out coefficient \(k_s = 0.195\) — activity coefficient in **seawater (μ = 0.7 M) deviates significantly from 1**; in **freshwater (μ = 0.001 M) it is approximately 1** → for dilute systems, activity ≈ concentration.

### S6. What is Photolysis and the relevant formula?
> Slides (Lecture 15 — Enthalpy application):
> - **Photolysis** = "when a molecule is dissociated by absorbing a photon of energy" — a photochemical reaction
> - For photolysis: photon energy must be ≥ ΔH of the reaction
> - **Energy of a photon:** \(E = hc/\lambda\), where:
>   - h = Planck's constant = **6.6 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s**
>   - c = speed of light = **3 × 10⁸ m/s**
>   - λ = wavelength (m)
> - Maximum wavelength for photolysis: \(\lambda \leq 1.19 \times 10^{-4} \text{ kJ·m/mol} / \Delta H \text{ (kJ/mol)}\)

**Spin-offs:**
- **Q:** Photolysis of Ozone — what is the maximum wavelength? (Slide example)
  **A:** Slides: Reaction: O₃ + hν → O₂ + O; ΔH = 247.5 − 142.9 = **104.6 kJ/mol** (endothermic — absorbs energy); \(\lambda \leq 1.19 \times 10^{-4}/104.6 = 1.13 \times 10^{-6} \text{ m} = \mathbf{1.13 \text{ μm}}\)
- **Q:** Why is it important that stratospheric ozone absorbs incoming solar radiation?
  **A:** Slides: Ozone photolysis (UV absorption) prevents high-energy radiation from reaching Earth's surface. The enthalpy change is **positive (endothermic)** — energy from the photon is consumed in breaking the O₃ bond.

### S7. Fukushima Example — Radioactive Decay (First-Order)
> Slides (Lecture 15 — half-life application):
> - Radioactive cesium: **t½ = 3 years**
> - Initial concentration: **>25,000 Bq/kg** in fish
> - Japan safety limit: **100 Bq/kg**; US threshold: **1,200 Bq/kg**
> - k = 0.693/3 = **0.231/year**
> - After 5 years: \(C = 25000 \times e^{-0.231 \times 5} = 25000 \times e^{-1.155} \approx 7,884 \text{ Bq/kg}\)
> - **Conclusion:** Still unsafe by both Japan (>100) and US (>1,200) limits

**Spin-offs:**
- **Q:** What is a becquerel (Bq)?
  **A:** Slides: **"1 becquerel = 1 radioactive disintegration per second."**
- **Q:** What formula is used to calculate cesium concentration after time t?
  **A:** Slides: First-order: \(C = C_0 \cdot e^{-kt}\), where \(k = 0.693/t_{1/2}\). This is the same integrated first-order rate law used for chemical degradation.
- **Q:** How many years would the Fukushima fish take to reach Japan's safety limit of 100 Bq/kg?
