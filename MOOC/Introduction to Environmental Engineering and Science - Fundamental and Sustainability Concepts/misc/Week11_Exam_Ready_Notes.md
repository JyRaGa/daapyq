### Q5. An industrial process emits 5 g of NO₂ every minute. Stack height = 40 m, zero plume rise, wind speed $u$ = 4 m/s, $\sigma_y$ = 30 m, $\sigma_z$ = 20 m. What is the ground-level centreline concentration 1 km downwind?

| Option | Text |
|--------|------|
| a | 0.5 µg/m³ |
| **b ✅** | **1.5 µg/m³** |
| c | 2.5 µg/m³ |
| d | 3.5 µg/m³ |

**✅ Correct Answer: b**

**Why b is correct:**
The ground-level centreline Gaussian dispersion formula (from slides):

$$C(x, 0, 0) = \frac{S}{\pi u \sigma_y \sigma_z} \exp\!\left(-\frac{y^2}{2\sigma_y^2}\right) \exp\!\left(-\frac{H^2}{2\sigma_z^2}\right)$$

At centreline, $y = 0$, so $\exp(-y^2/2\sigma_y^2) = 1$.

Converting emission rate: $S = 5\ \text{g/min} \times \frac{1\ \text{min}}{60\ \text{s}} = \frac{1}{12}\ \text{g/s}$

$$C = \frac{(1/12)}{\pi \times 4 \times 30 \times 20} \times \exp\!\left(-\frac{40^2}{2 \times 20^2}\right)$$

$$= \frac{0.0833}{7539.8} \times \exp(-2) = 1.105 \times 10^{-5} \times 0.1353 \approx 1.5 \times 10^{-6}\ \text{g/m}^3 = 1.5\ \mu\text{g/m}^3$$

**Why others are wrong:**
- **a** — 0.5 µg/m³ would require a smaller emission rate or larger dispersion coefficients.
- **c, d** — These would result from arithmetic errors (e.g., forgetting the $\exp(-H^2/2\sigma_z^2)$ term which reduces concentration significantly).

#### Type 2 — Concept Expansion Spin-offs (Q5)

**Q5-C1.** In the Gaussian plume model, the full 3D concentration equation is:

$$C(x,y,z) = \frac{S}{2\pi u \sigma_y \sigma_z} \exp\!\left(-\frac{y^2}{2\sigma_y^2}\right)\left\{\exp\!\left(-\frac{(z-H)^2}{2\sigma_z^2}\right) + \exp\!\left(-\frac{(z+H)^2}{2\sigma_z^2}\right)\right\}$$

The ground-level formula (setting $z = 0$) compared to this uses a coefficient of:
- a) $\frac{S}{4\pi u \sigma_y \sigma_z}$
- **b) $\frac{S}{\pi u \sigma_y \sigma_z}$ ✅**

- d) $\frac{2S}{\pi u \sigma_y \sigma_z}$

> *Slides: at $z = 0$, the two exponential terms in $z$ are equal so they combine: coefficient becomes $S/(\pi u \sigma_y \sigma_z)$.*

### Q7. Under which atmospheric condition is vertical movement of an air parcel discouraged?

| Option | Text |
|--------|------|
| a | Unstable conditions |
| b | Neutral stability |
| **c ✅** | **Inversion conditions** |
| d | Turbulent conditions |

**✅ Correct Answer: c**

**Why c is correct:**
Slides state: *"During stable conditions, vertical movement of an air parcel is discouraged. Under very stable conditions, a cooler layer of air near the land surface is capped by an upper warmer air layer. This condition is called an inversion and prevents vertical motion of an air parcel."* Inversion = most stable atmospheric condition.

**Why others are wrong:**
- **a** — **Unstable conditions** actively *encourage* vertical motion — a rising air parcel remains warmer than its surroundings and continues to rise (looping plume).
- **b** — **Neutral stability** allows vertical movement proportional to mechanical (wind-driven) turbulence — it neither encourages nor strongly discourages it.
- **d** — **Turbulent conditions** are associated with unstable/windy weather — they promote vigorous vertical mixing, not suppression.

### Q14. Match the environmental conditions to the shape of the plume.

| Condition | Plume |
|-----------|-------|
| (i) Unstable | ? |
| (ii) Stable (inversion) | ? |
| (iii) Inversion aloft | ? |
| (iv) Inversion at ground | ? |

| Option | Matching |
|--------|----------|
| **a ✅** | **(i) b-Looping, (ii) d-Fanning, (iii) a-Fumigating, (iv) c-Lofting** |
| b | (i) c, (ii) a, (iii) d, (iv) b |
| c | (i) a, (ii) b, (iii) d, (iv) c |
| d | (i) b, (ii) a, (iii) c, (iv) d |

**✅ Correct Answer: a**

**Why a is correct (slide reference: Lectures 74–78):**

| Plume Type | Condition | Mechanism |
|------------|-----------|-----------|
| **Looping** | Unstable | Strong vertical turbulence → plume loops up and down |
| **Fanning** | Stable/inversion | Very little vertical mixing → plume stays flat, fans out horizontally |
| **Fumigating** | Inversion aloft | Plume released just below inversion; unstable air below forces it to ground |
| **Lofting** | Inversion at ground | Stable layer near ground + unstable air above → plume rises, cannot reach ground |

**Why others are wrong:**
All other options mix up the fanning/looping or fumigating/lofting assignments, which have distinct atmospheric stability conditions as shown above.
