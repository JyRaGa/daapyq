## 🚨 SECTION 1: THE FINAL EXAM TRAP LIST

- **The SO₂ GHG Trap:** Sulphur Dioxide (SO₂) is a major air pollutant and causes acid rain, but it is **NOT** a greenhouse gas.
- **The Secondary Criteria Trap:** Ozone (O₃) is the **ONLY** criteria pollutant that has no direct emissions; it is 100% secondary.
- **The Stability Trap:**
  - If **ELR > DALR**: Atmosphere is **Unstable** (Good for dispersion).
  - If **ELR < DALR**: Atmosphere is **Stable** (Bad for dispersion, leads to smog).
  - If **ELR = DALR**: Atmosphere is **Neutral**.
- **The PM Size Trap:** PM₂.₅ is more dangerous than PM₁₀ because it can reach the **deepest part of the lungs** (alveoli) and enter the bloodstream.
- **The Nitric Acid Trap (Slide Veto!):** Scientifically, Nitric Acid (HNO₃) is a secondary pollutant. However, **Slide 4 of the instructor deck explicitly lists HNO₃ as a PRIMARY pollutant**. If this appears in an MCQ, pick **"Primary"** to match the course materials.
- **The Rural Stove Trap:** Concentrations of air pollution from rural stoves using biomass cakes are **5 times higher** than those from coal.

## 💨 SECTION 2: TYPES OF AIR POLLUTANTS

**1. Primary vs. Secondary Pollutants:**

- **Primary:** Emitted directly from the source (like throwing trash out the window).
  - Examples: Smoke, Dust, Carbon Monoxide (CO), Sulphur Dioxide (SO₂), Nitric Oxide (NO), **Nitric Acid (HNO₃) per Slide 4**.
- **Secondary:** Formed when primary pollutants react in sunlight (like baking a cake in the atmosphere).
  - Examples: Smog, Ozone (O₃), Sulphur Trioxide (SO₃).

**Particulate Matter Definitions:**

- **Aerosols:** A general term for any dispersion of solid or liquid particles in a gaseous medium.
- **Dust:** Formed by **mechanical action** (grinding/crushing).
- **Fumes:** Formed by **condensation of vapors** (usually from chemical reactions or distillation).

**2. The 6 Criteria Pollutants ("The Big Bad Six"):**

The government sets specific NAAQS limits for: **CO, SO₂, NO₂, PM₁₀ and PM₂.₅, Lead (Pb), and Ozone (O₃)**.

- **Ultimate Trap:** Ozone (O₃) is the **ONLY** criteria pollutant with **NO direct emissions** — created entirely by VOCs + NOₓ + Sunlight.

**3. Specific Pollutant Hazards:**

- **Lead (Pb):** Destroys the developing nervous systems of children.
- **Carbon Monoxide (CO):** Invisible, odorless gas that binds to haemoglobin **200× faster than oxygen**, causing asphyxiation (carboxyhemoglobin formation).
- **SO₂:** Mixes with water to create **Acid Rain (H₂SO₄)**. NOT a greenhouse gas.

**PM₁₀ Emission Hierarchy** (decreasing order of PM₁₀ emissions):

> **Crop Residue > Wood > Coal > Natural Gas > Electricity**

**WHO Air Quality Statistics (Memorise):**

- **91%** of the world's population lives in areas exceeding WHO air quality limits.
- **4.2 million** deaths/year from ambient (outdoor) air pollution.
- **3.8 million** deaths/year from household (indoor) air pollution.
- **45%** of pneumonia deaths in children under 5 are linked to household air pollution.
- **28%** of adult pneumonia deaths are linked to household air pollution.

## 🌡️ SECTION 3: ATMOSPHERIC STABILITY & PLUME TYPES

**5. The Lapse Rate:**

- **Dry Adiabatic Lapse Rate (DALR):** A constant law of physics — a dry rising air bubble cools at exactly **9.8°C per kilometre**. (Descending air heats at the same rate.)
- **Environmental Lapse Rate (ELR):** The *actual* temperature profile of the atmosphere right now, measured by a weather balloon.
  - **Standard ELR Range:** The average ELR in the troposphere is **6 – 7°C/km**. The "Standard Atmosphere" value is often cited as **6.5°C/km**.

**Stability Rules:**

| Condition | Rule | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Unstable** | ELR > DALR | Smoke keeps rising — good dispersion |
| **Neutral** | ELR = DALR | Smoke and sky match temperatures |
| **Stable (Inversion)** | ELR < DALR | Smoke trapped near ground — dangerous |

**6. The 5 Plume Types:**

| Plume | Condition | Visual Shape | Danger Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Looping** | Highly unstable (ELR >> DALR) | Wavy roller-coaster | Medium (may touch ground) |
| **Coning** | Neutral (ELR = DALR) | Perfect trumpet/cone | Low |
| **Fanning** | Strong inversion | Flat horizontal pancake | Low at ground, bad overall |
| **Lofting** | Inversion *below* stack | Flat bottom, puffy top | **Best** — goes up, stays up |
| **Fumigation** | Inversion *above* stack | Flat top, puffy bottom | **Worst** — forced to ground |

## 🏭 SECTION 4: AIR POLLUTION CONTROL TECHNOLOGIES/devices

**7. Particulate Matter (PM) Control:**
* **6. Catching Dust (Particulate Matter Control):**
    * **Gravity Settling Chamber:** A giant room where heavy dust just falls to the floor.
    * **Cyclone Separator:** Creates a mini-tornado inside a cone. Centrifugal force throws the dust against the walls.
    * **Baghouse Filter:** A giant vacuum cleaner with huge fabric socks that catch the dust.
    * **Electrostatic Precipitator (ESP):** Uses high-voltage electricity to give the dust a static charge, then pulls it toward a giant magnet plate. Highly efficient for very fine dust!
* **7. Catching Gases (Gas Control):**
    * **Wet Scrubber:** Spraying a chemical rain shower inside a tower to "wash" the toxic gas out of the smoke.


| Device | Principle | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Gravity Settling Chamber** | Gravity only | Large particles only (> 50 µm) |
| **Cyclone Separator** | Centrifugal force (mini-tornado) | Medium particles |
| **Baghouse Filter** | Fabric filtration (giant vacuum sock) | Fine PM, high efficiency |
| **Electrostatic Precipitator (ESP)** | High-voltage charging + attraction plate | Very fine PM, **99%+ efficiency** |

**8. Gaseous Pollutant Control:**

- **Absorption (Wet Scrubbers):** Spray a chemical/water rain shower inside a tower to "wash" toxic gases (e.g., SO₂) out of the exhaust.
- **Adsorption:** Uses solid surfaces like **Activated Carbon** to stick to and capture pollutants.
- **Venturi Scrubber (Dual-Purpose — 2024 MSQ Trap!):** A type of wet scrubber that removes **both Particulate Matter** (via inertial impaction) **AND soluble gases like SO₂ and HCl** (via absorption) in a single unit.

## 🔢 SECTION 7: NUMERICAL MASTER-BANK

### Gaussian Plume Dispersion

**The Simplified Ground-Level Centreline Formula** (z = 0, y = 0):

```
C = (S / (π · u · σy · σz)) × exp(−H² / 2σz²)
```

Where:
- `C` = ground-level concentration (µg/m³)
- `S` = emission rate (**must be in µg/s**)
- `u` = wind speed (m/s)
- `σy`, `σz` = horizontal and vertical dispersion coefficients (m)
- `H` = effective stack height (m)

**🚨 Unit Conversion Trap:** Always convert emission rate to **µg/s** before plugging in.

**Worked Example:**

- Emission rate: 2.4 g/min → 2.4/60 = 0.04 g/s → **40,000 µg/s**
- H = 15 m, u = 3 m/s, σy = 25 m, σz = 15 m

**Step 1 — Denominator:**
```
π × 3 × 25 × 15 = 3534.29
```

**Step 2 — Exponent:**
```
−H² / (2σz²) = −225 / 450 = −0.5
exp(−0.5) = 0.6065
```

**Step 3 — Final answer:**
```
C = (40,000 / 3534.29) × 0.6065 = 11.317 × 0.6065 ≈ 6.86 µg/m³
```
*(Rounded in slides to **6.9 µg/m³**)*

## 🚀 SECTION 9: ACTIVE RECALL & FEYNMAN CHALLENGE

*Cover the answers below and test yourself before the exam.*

**Quiz 1:** A factory is emitting smoke during a sunny afternoon with high atmospheric instability. What plume shape will form, and is this good or bad for people on the ground?

> **Answer:** Looping. It is generally good because the smoke disperses rapidly upward, but because of the turbulent up-and-down motion, it may occasionally dip and touch the ground momentarily.

**Quiz 2:** The government measures the ELR and finds that temperature is *increasing* as altitude increases. What type of atmospheric condition is this? Which plume type is most dangerous in this scenario?

> **Answer:** This is a Temperature Inversion (Stable condition, ELR < DALR). The most dangerous plume in this scenario is **Fumigation**, where the inversion layer above the stack forces all pollutants straight down onto the city.

**Feynman Challenge:** Explain how an Electrostatic Precipitator (ESP) works to a 10-year-old.

> **Example Answer:** Imagine you have a room full of floating dust. Now imagine you have a magic wand that zaps every dust particle with static electricity — like rubbing a balloon on your hair. Once all the dust is charged up, you put a giant magnet plate on the wall. All the charged dust particles fly toward the plate and stick to it. That's exactly what an ESP does — it charges the dust and then pulls it out of the smoke with an electrically charged collection plate, leaving clean air behind!
