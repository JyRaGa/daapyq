## 📌 TOPIC 1 — Why This Course Exists (Context)

**🟡 MEDIUM Importance**

### The Core Problem

Humans consume **>50 kg of resources per person per day**, yet only 3 kg gets recycled. Urban areas cover only ~1% of land surface but produce **60–70% of all greenhouse gas emissions**.[^1]

The **IPAT Equation** captures this neatly:

$$
I = P \times A \times T
$$

Where:

- **I** = Environmental Impact
- **P** = Population
- **A** = Affluence (consumption per person)
- **T** = Technology (impact per unit of consumption)

> 🔑 Before 1800, human impact was **localised**. After 1800 (Industrial Revolution), it became **GLOBAL**. The equation's variables all exploded simultaneously.[^1]

## 📌 TOPIC 2 — Defining Sustainability

**🔴 HIGH Importance**

### The Triple Bottom Line — Most Important Concept

Sustainability sits at the intersection of **3 pillars** (know the Venn diagram intersections!):[^1]


| Intersection | What It's Called |
| :-- | :-- |
| Environment + Society | **Bearable** |
| Environment + Economy | **Viable** |
| Society + Economy | **Equitable** |
| All Three Together | **SUSTAINABLE** ✅ |

### Three Key Definitions (Memorize All Three!)

**1. Sustainability** (Merriam-Webster):
> A method of harvesting/using a resource so it is **not depleted or permanently damaged**

**2. Sustainable Development** (Brundtland Commission, 1987):
> "Development which **meets the needs of the present** without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their needs"

**3. Sustainable Engineering**:
> Design of human and industrial systems to ensure that **use of natural resources does not lead to diminished quality of life** due to losses in future economic opportunities or adverse impacts on health/environment[^1]

> ⚠️ **Trap:** Many students only memorize the Brundtland definition. Exams often test all three definitions separately — especially Sustainable Engineering.

### Ecological Footprint

The **land area + water** required to sustainably produce all resources a population needs.[^1]

- Vancouver (1.7M people): footprint = **19× its actual area**
- Netherlands: **14× its area**
- **Earth Overshoot Day**: In 1970 we needed 1 Earth/year. By 2018, we needed **1.7 Earths/year**[^1]


### Tragedy of the Commons

Garrett Hardin's theory: individuals acting **rationally in self-interest** collectively destroy shared resources (fisheries, atmosphere, rainforests).[^1]

### Carrying Capacity

The **upper limit** of population size that an environment can support sustainably — set by availability of food, space, and renewable resources.[^1]

## 📌 TOPIC 4 — Engineering for Sustainability

**🔴 HIGH Importance**

### 3 Levels of Design (Know the Hierarchy!)

This is a **very commonly tested concept** — often as MCQ or short answer:[^1]


| Level | Approach | Example (Car Mileage) |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **1. Incremental Improvement** | Optimize existing solution | Lighter materials, better engine efficiency |
| **2. Reengineer the System** | Redesign how it works | Hybrid/electric cars, aerodynamic shapes |
| **3. Redefine the System Boundary** | Question if the thing is needed | Public transit, walkable cities — eliminate the car entirely |

> 🔑 Level 3 has the **maximum sustainability benefit** but requires the most change. This is the key insight.

### 12 Principles of Green Chemistry (Know a Few!)

Framework for designing chemicals/processes to minimize hazard and waste:[^1]

1. Prevent waste · 2. Atom Economy · 3. Less hazardous synthesis · 4. Design benign chemicals · 5. Benign solvents · 6. Energy efficiency · 7. Renewable feedstocks · 8. Reduce derivatives · 9. Catalysis · 10. Design for degradation · 11. Real-time pollution analysis · 12. Inherently safe chemistry

> Exam tip: They may ask which principle covers "design chemicals to break down after use" → **Principle 10** (Design for Degradation)

## 📌 TOPIC 5 — Life Cycle Thinking, LCA \& Circular Economy

**🔴 HIGH Importance**

### Life Cycle Thinking vs System Thinking

| Framework | Focus | What It Captures |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Life Cycle Thinking** | Material \& energy flows | Environmental impacts at each stage of a product's life |
| **System Thinking** | Broader relationships | Also includes political, cultural, social, economic feedbacks |

### 4 Phases of LCA (Life Cycle Assessment)

$$
\text{Goal \& Scope} \leftrightarrow \text{Inventory Analysis} \leftrightarrow \text{Impact Assessment} \leftrightarrow \text{Interpretation}
$$

All four phases **interact with each other bidirectionally** — this is often the basis of an MCQ trap ("which phase comes last?"). The answer is **Interpretation**, but it connects to all others.[^1]

**Real Examples from Slides:**

- **Aluminium**: Extraction/smelting is most impactful → that's why recycling aluminium saves huge energy
- **Automobile**: Use phase (combustion + road runoff) creates most impact
- **Building**: Operations phase (heating/cooling) creates most impact
- **CFL vs LED**: CFL saves energy but contains **4 mg mercury** per bulb — LCT reveals the trade-off[^1]


### Circular Economy vs Linear Economy

| Linear Economy | Circular Economy |
| :-- | :-- |
| Make → Use → **Dispose** (throwaway) | Keep resources in use as long as possible |
| Value lost at end of life | Value recovered, regenerated |
| One-time resource use | Closed-loop material flow |

### E-Factor Formula (Quantitative!)

$$
E\text{-factor} = \frac{\sum \text{kg inputs}}{\sum \text{kg desired product}}
$$

**Lower E-factor = more sustainable process** (less waste per kg of product)[^1]

> 🔑 E-factor is a **material efficiency metric** — it measures waste generation, not energy or toxicity.

### Sustainability Indicators

Traditional indicators measure what exists. Sustainable indicators measure **activities that cause problems**.[^1]


| Type | Traditional | Sustainable |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Economic | GDP, median income | Wages spent locally; hours to meet basic needs |
| Environmental | Pollution levels | Vehicle miles traveled; % renewable energy |
| Social | SAT scores, voters registered | % who actually vote; job-skill matching |

### Policies Driving Sustainability

1. **Regulations** — e.g., Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR), bans on plastics
2. **Voluntary Programs** — e.g., eco-labeling, preferential purchasing[^1]

## ⚠️ Common Mistakes \& Traps

1. **Brundtland = Sustainable Development**, NOT sustainability. These are two separate definitions.
2. **Triple Bottom Line intersections**: Students mix up Bearable/Viable/Equitable. Remember: **E+S = Bearable** (two non-money things); **Eco+Env = Viable** (money + nature); **Eco+Soc = Equitable** (money + people).
3. **MDGs ≠ SDGs**: MDGs = 8 goals (2000–2015). SDGs = 17 goals (2016–present). Not the same list.
4. **E-factor**: It's inputs ÷ product, not product ÷ inputs. Higher = MORE waste.
5. **LCA phases**: All four phases interact bidirectionally — don't assume a strict linear sequence.
6. **Carrying capacity trap**: It refers to environmental limits on population, NOT just food supply.

## 🧮 New Solved Examples

**Example A — E-factor:**
A chemical process uses 5 kg butanol + 10 kg NaBr + 8 kg H₂SO₄ and produces 12 kg desired product. Find E-factor.

$$
E\text{-factor} = \frac{5 + 10 + 8}{12} = \frac{23}{12} \approx \mathbf{1.92}
$$

For every 1 kg of product, 0.92 kg is "waste" — moderate efficiency.

**Example B — IPAT:**
If population doubles, affluence grows 1.5×, but technology improves to halve impact per unit — net environmental impact = ?

$$
I_{new} = 2P \times 1.5A \times 0.5T = 1.5 \times I_{original}
$$

→ Impact still **increases by 50%** even with tech improvement. This is why efficiency alone isn't enough![^1]
